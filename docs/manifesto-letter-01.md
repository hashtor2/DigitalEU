# Why We Built digitaleu.me — And Why Your Inbox Is the Best Place to Start

*The first article from digitaleu.me*

Almost every European who starts paying attention to where their data goes has the same small moment. It isn't dramatic. You don't get hacked, nobody steals your identity. You just open your email one evening, search for "welcome to," and watch the results keep scrolling. Netflix, Spotify, Amazon, Google, Meta. Fifteen years of small, reasonable decisions, each one handing a sliver of your life to a company headquartered an ocean away, governed by laws you never voted on, running a business model you never actually agreed to.

That moment, multiplied across tens of millions of people, is why we built digitaleu.me.

## The problem isn't any single company. It's the architecture.

It's become fashionable to talk about "Big Tech" as a villain, and there's no shortage of grievances to list. But the problem we actually care about isn't that any one company is malicious. It's that a whole generation of European digital life got built on infrastructure designed somewhere else, optimised for a different legal system, and increasingly shaped by decisions made far outside Brussels, Berlin, or Oslo.

When your email, your storage, your calendar, your VPN, your password manager, and your browser all sit on infrastructure controlled by a handful of American firms — subject to American law, American subpoenas, American corporate strategy — you don't own your digital life. You rent it. On terms you never read.

This isn't a worry that only makes sense at a privacy conference. It shows up in concrete ways: exposure under the U.S. CLOUD Act, content-moderation policies that shift overnight, prices set on another continent, and the slow realisation that the infrastructure European institutions and individuals depend on answers to someone else's law first.

Europe has built real alternatives. Companies that encrypt by default, that treat GDPR as a founding principle rather than a compliance chore, that have made a business out of *not* selling your attention. Proton, Tuta, Mullvad, Nextcloud, and a long list of others built by people who decided privacy shouldn't be a paid upgrade.

So the alternatives were never the missing piece. The switching cost was.

## Why nobody switches, even when they want to

Ask people whether they'd prefer a privacy-respecting European email provider to a Big Tech one and a lot of them say yes, easily. Then nothing happens. People aren't loyal to Gmail. They're stuck to it.

And they're stuck for a boring reason. To switch, you have to work out which of the forty-odd services you signed up for over the years still send you anything that matters. Then you log into each one, dig through the account settings, change the address, confirm it, and hope you didn't forget the one tied to your mortgage or your kid's school portal. It's not hard the way learning a language is hard. It's hard the way paperwork is hard: tedious, easy to put off, individually small enough that "I'll do it next weekend" quietly becomes never.

That gap, between wanting to leave Big Tech and actually doing it, is most of why the move to European tools has been so much slower than people's stated preferences would suggest.

digitaleu.me exists to close it.

## What we're actually building

digitaleu.me is a migration portal. The point is to do the unglamorous work of leaving Big Tech *for* you, instead of telling you that you really should.

The first tool is an inbox scanner, and it runs entirely on your device. Your email content never reaches any server, including ours. It reads only the metadata it needs to work out which services you've signed up with, and nothing beyond that. Then it hands you a dashboard you can actually use: here's everything tied to this address, here's what each service does, and here's a European alternative wherever a good one exists.

Alongside it is a browser extension that does one small thing well. Once you've decided to move an account to a new address, it fills that address in for you on each site, so updating dozens of accounts doesn't turn into a second job.

We built the sensitive parts — the scanning, the account detection — to happen on your own machine on purpose. If you choose to save your progress between sessions, that's the only point where anything gets stored, and it's encrypted on your device before it ever leaves it. The server it lands on sits in Switzerland, which holds an EU adequacy decision and some of the strongest data-protection guarantees anywhere. We didn't want to be in a position where we *could* read your data even if we never would. The architecture should make that promise, not the privacy policy.

## We are not building a Proton fan page

The easy version of this is a thin affiliate site that points everyone at two or three famous European brands and collects a commission. We're not doing that, and we think you'd see through it in about a minute anyway.

The European tech ecosystem is far bigger than the same handful of names that turn up in every "privacy alternatives" listicle. There are whole categories — email, cloud storage, VPNs, password managers, browsers, search, productivity suites, payments, analytics — where competitive and sometimes flat-out better European options exist and just lack the marketing budget of their American rivals. Our job is to surface those honestly, tradeoffs included, instead of pretending every alternative is perfect because it happens to be European.

That standard applies to us too. Right now parts of our own stack still run on non-European infrastructure, because in these early days it's faster. We're not going to hide that. Every one of those choices is logged in the open as something we mean to revisit, especially before we lean hard on a sovereignty message we haven't fully earned yet. On this topic credibility comes from example, not slogans.

## Why this matters beyond a tidy inbox

You could read all of this as a convenience story. Declutter your inbox, tidy your digital footprint, feel a little better about where your data lives. That's true, and it's reason enough to use the tool on its own.

But there's a bigger pattern underneath. Digital sovereignty has stopped being an abstract phrase argued over in committee rooms and turned into something people and businesses feel directly: in outages, in pricing decided elsewhere, in the dawning sense that infrastructure critical to European life runs through companies whose first loyalty lies somewhere else.

We don't think every American product is suspect, and we've no interest in nationalism wearing a privacy costume. What we believe is narrower: a healthy digital society needs real optionality. Usable alternatives that keep the dominant players honest, keep critical infrastructure diversified, and give people a genuine choice instead of a default they never actively picked. The alternatives mostly exist. The bridge between knowing about them and moving to them is what's been missing, and that bridge — boring, practical, account by account — is what we're building.

## What's next

This launch is small on purpose. The scanner and the early dashboard cover a focused set of strong alternatives across email, storage, and a few neighbouring categories. Not because that's the ceiling of our ambition, but because we'd rather ship something solid than something broad and half-finished.

From here we expand into a wider, carefully curated catalogue, with practical comparison guides. We'll start with browsers, since that's one of the most consequential choices most people make without thinking about it at all. Further out we're looking hard at the B2B side, where the stakes and the compliance reasons for caring are higher still.

All of it rests on getting the boring part right first. Make leaving easy. Make the alternative obvious. Turn sovereignty into a few clicks instead of a weekend project nobody ever starts.

That's digitaleu.me. Built by people who think Europeans deserve to own their digital lives, and who got tired of watching good intentions die in the gap between caring and doing.

If that lands, the inbox scanner is where to start. About two minutes to see exactly where your data lives, and what it'd take to bring it home.
