# Why We Built digitaleu.me — And Why Your Inbox Is the Best Place to Start

*The first article from digitaleu.me*

Almost every European who starts paying attention to where their data goes has the same small moment. It isn't dramatic. You just open your email one evening, search for "welcome to," and watch the results keep scrolling. Netflix, Spotify, Amazon, Google, Meta. Fifteen years of small, reasonable decisions, each one handing a sliver of your life to a company an ocean away, governed by laws you never voted on, running a business model you never agreed to.

That moment, multiplied across tens of millions of people, is why we built digitaleu.me.

## The problem isn't any one company. It's the architecture.

When your email, your storage, your calendar, your password manager, and your browser all sit on infrastructure controlled by a handful of American firms — subject to American law and the U.S. CLOUD Act — you don't own your digital life. You rent it. On terms you never read. It shows up in concrete ways: data exposed to foreign subpoenas, content rules that shift overnight, prices set on another continent.

Europe has built real alternatives. Companies that encrypt by default, that treat GDPR as a founding principle rather than a compliance chore, that make a business out of *not* selling your attention. Proton, Tuta, Mullvad, Nextcloud, and a long list of others built by people who decided privacy shouldn't be a paid upgrade.

The alternatives were never the missing piece. The switching cost was.

## Why nobody switches, even when they want to

Ask people whether they'd prefer a private, European email provider to a Big Tech one and most say yes, easily. Then nothing happens. People aren't loyal to Gmail. They're stuck to it.

And they're stuck for a boring reason. To switch, you have to work out which of the forty-odd services you signed up for over the years still send you anything that matters, log into each one, dig through the settings, change the address, confirm it, and hope you didn't forget the one tied to your mortgage or your kid's school portal. It's hard the way paperwork is hard: tedious, easy to put off, small enough that "next weekend" quietly becomes never.

That gap is what we exist to close.

## What we're building, and how we protect it

digitaleu.me is a migration portal that does the unglamorous work of leaving Big Tech *for* you.

The first tool is an inbox scanner, and it runs entirely on your device. Your email content never reaches any server, including ours. It reads only the metadata it needs to see which services you've signed up with, then hands you a clear dashboard: here's everything tied to this address, here's what each service does, and here's a European, privacy-respecting alternative for each one.

A browser extension does the rest. Once you've chosen a new address, it fills that address in for you across each site, so updating dozens of accounts doesn't become a second job.

Privacy isn't a feature we bolted on; it's the architecture. The scanning happens on your machine. If you save your progress, it's encrypted on your device before it ever leaves, and the only server involved sits in Switzerland, under an EU adequacy decision and some of the strongest data-protection law anywhere. We never want to be in a position where we *could* read your data, even if we never would. The design makes that promise, not the privacy policy.

## A real catalogue, not a shortlist

The European tech ecosystem is far bigger than the same handful of names in every "privacy alternatives" listicle. Email, cloud storage, VPNs, password managers, browsers, search, productivity suites — whole categories where competitive, often better European options exist and just lack the marketing budget of their American rivals. Our job is to surface them honestly, tradeoffs included, not to crown a winner because it happens to be European.

## Start with your inbox

Europe already has the tools. What's been missing is the bridge between knowing about them and actually moving — boring, practical, account by account. That's what we built.

The inbox scanner is where it starts. About two minutes to see exactly where your data lives, and what it'd take to bring it home.
